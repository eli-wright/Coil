v.  Field Notes for the Refrigeration Tech

Reference · 05

What the calculation does not model — but the field engineer must specify before fabrication and operation.

Vessel Internals

Oil drain & control

Oil migrates to the bottom of the bath and forms an insulating layer over the lower coil turns. Specify an oil drain valve at the lowest point and a procedure for periodic removal (typically every 1–3 months in screw-compressor systems). Oil contamination can reduce ho by 30 – 50 %, pushing the predicted outlet warmer by 4–8 °F.

Coil supports

Industrial spiral coils need vertical support bars welded every 4–6 turns, typically 3 to 4 bars spaced around the circumference. The bars occupy ~ ½ in of envelope each and may interfere with the radial clearance margin. Account for them in fabrication drawings.

Nozzle orientation

Liquid inlet at the top (or upper side) of the vessel, outlet at the bottom side — gives countercurrent-like exposure and avoids vapor pocket trapping at the top of the coil. Orient both nozzles on the same side of the vessel for ease of insulation and field piping.
Material & Code

Pipe material specification

For bath temperatures down to −20 °F: ASTM A106 Gr. B carbon steel meets ASME B31.5 toughness requirements. Below −20 °F: switch to A333 Gr. 6 (low-temperature carbon steel) and specify Charpy impact testing per code. All welds NDT to 100 % per IIAR 2 (R-717) or ASHRAE 15 / ASME B31.5 practice for other refrigerants.

Pressure rating

HP liquid lines see condensing pressure — design for MAWP at least equal to your system's high-side relief setting; typically ≥ 250 psig for ammonia condensing service. Standard carbon-steel Sch 40/80 piping carries generous pressure capacity at HP-liquid temperatures, but the actual rating depends on material grade, joint design, temperature, corrosion allowance, and the applicable piping code.

Verify pipe MAWP separately per ASME B31.5 (refrigeration piping) and any applicable jurisdictional requirements (IIAR 2 for R-717, ASHRAE 15 for halocarbon/CO₂/hydrocarbon systems, local AHJ). HELIX does not compute or certify pipe pressure rating.

Operations

Liquid level swell

During pull-down or rapid load increase, the bath level can swell 4 – 8 in upward as bubbles fill the liquid mass. Conversely, during defrost or partial-load operation the level can drop. The 6 in swell allowance baked into the submergence calculation covers normal operation; for systems with rapid load cycling, consider 8 in.

Start-up behavior

At cold start, the bath is not yet at saturation and the coil delivers less subcooling than design. Expect outlet temperatures up to inlet temperature for the first 5 – 15 minutes until the bath reaches stable suction temperature. The downstream TXV or hand expansion valve must be sized to tolerate this transient.

Instrumentation

Minimum field instruments:
  • TI at coil outlet, immediately downstream of last bend (within 5 pipe diameters)
  • TI on the bath at coil-elevation centerline
  • PI at coil inlet and outlet (or single ΔP transmitter)
  • LI on the vessel for level control
Performance Reality

What HELIX models — and what it does not

Modeled: Steady-state heat transfer; Dittus-Boelter tube side with selectable Schmidt or Kern helical correction and optional Sieder-Tate viscosity correction; shell-side mode-selectable between (a) T-dependent pool-boiling correlation calibrated to three industrial R-717 reference designs (Mostinski reduced-pressure scaling for other refrigerants), (b) static-bath conservative ho, and (c) Patil/Shende/Ghosh 1982 forced annular flow; Ito helical pressure drop; geometric clearance; 3–7 ft/s liquid velocity check; CoolProp (Helmholtz-EOS) refrigerant properties for all ten selectable fluids; basic water/glycol/brine property tables for secondary-fluid services.

Not modeled: Two-phase refrigerant in the coil (assumes always single-phase liquid), partial submergence, oil migration dynamics, vibration analysis, support-bar effects on envelope, transient warm-up, non-condensables in the bath, coil-to-vessel weld stress.

Confidence band: Reproduces three independent industrial R-717 reference calculations within ±0.2 °F outlet temperature; for other refrigerants the shell-side correlation is a scaled estimate without field calibration. In the field, expect ±5 °F uncertainty due to fouling, oil, and instrument accuracy.

v.  Field Notes for Boil-out Service

Reference · 05

Service-specific guidance for periodic boil-out coils. What HELIX models well, what it approximates conservatively, and what the technician must specify before fabrication and operation.

Service envelope

What the three presets cover

  • Oil Boil-out — periodic warm-up (20–120 min) to ~+35 to +55 °F to vaporize entrained refrigerant so the carried compressor oil drops to a separator. Coil valved off most of the time.
  • Defrost / Bath Warm-up — quick heat injection (10–60 min) to thaw adjacent equipment. Heating fluid is usually hot discharge gas.
  • Pull-down Pre-warm — longer cycle (30–180 min) to warm a cold vessel before pump-down so internal moisture doesn't freeze on the shell during evacuation.

Calibration limits

The shell-side pool-boiling correlation is calibrated for R-717 at bath T = −25 to +30 °F (other refrigerants use a Mostinski reduced-pressure scaling of that base). Boil-out cycles routinely warm the bath above +30 °F, putting part of the cycle outside the calibrated range. HELIX flags this with the orange calibration banner on the Sizer tab. Confidence: moderate. Recommended: validate the first installation against measured cycle time before relying on this tool for production sizing.
Heating fluid physics

HP Liquid R-717 mode

Warm HP liquid from the receiver (condensing temperature, typically +85…+95 °F). Tube-side hi via Dittus-Boelter (n=0.3, cooling) + Schmidt helical correction. Liquid is dense and high-cp, so a modest mass flow delivers high duty. Velocity guideline: 3–7 ft/s (industry liquid-line range; IIAR for R-717).

Hot Gas R-717 mode

Hot compressor discharge gas at the fluid's typical high-side pressure (dew pressure at 95 °F condensing; ~170 psia for R-717, far higher for R-410A / R-744). Tube-side modeled as single-phase superheated vapor at the inlet T. Velocity guideline: 15–80 ft/s (gas range).

Conservative approximation. Real hot-gas defrost involves condensation on the inner wall as the gas cools below saturation at line pressure. Condensing hi is 5–10× higher than single-phase vapor convection. Actual cycle times in the field will be 30–50% faster than HELIX predicts. Predictions are safe for sizing (you'll never under-design); they are biased pessimistic.

Operations

Asymptotic cycles & system isolation

If heat-loss to ambient exceeds heat-in from the coil, the bath asymptotes below the target and HELIX reports "cannot reach target". Two common causes: insulation degraded, or target T set too close to ambient. The boil-out coil only needs to overpower the standing heat loss plus the sensible energy to raise the bath. Verify your heat-loss U value against actual insulation condition.

Valving & isolation

Boil-out coils are valved off during normal operation — when the system is in cooling mode the coil sits dry of heating fluid. Isolation valves on both inlet and outlet, with a small bleed/equalization line to handle thermal expansion of any trapped liquid. Coordinate with the system schematic; the HPR or hot-gas header must have capacity to supply the coil during the cycle without starving primary cooling.

System absorption

The heat injected during a boil-out cycle generates vapor in the bath that the compressors must absorb. Peak vapor generation is ~Qpeak / hfg. R-717 example: for a 60-TR peak duty on a +30 °F bath (hfg ≈ 543 Btu/lb), that's ~22 lb/min of additional vapor — low-latent-heat halocarbons generate 5–8× the mass flow for the same duty — typically a manageable transient if the compressors have headroom, but it should be checked against system capacity, especially in high-utilization plants.
Material & code

Pipe material

Boil-out coils see warm tube fluid in a cold bath at the start of the cycle, then progressively warming bath. For initial bath ≥ −20 °F: ASTM A106 Gr. B is acceptable. For initial bath below −20 °F (typical pull-down on a low-temp system): use A333 Gr. 6 with Charpy impact testing. All welds NDT per IIAR 2.

Verify pipe MAWP separately per ASME B31.5 (refrigeration piping) and applicable AHJ. Hot-gas mode sees discharge pressure and HP-liquid mode sees receiver pressure — both set by the refrigerant's condensing pressure (R-717 ~150–250 psig; R-410A ~400+ psig; R-744 can exceed 1,000 psig). HELIX does not compute or certify pipe pressure rating.

What HELIX models — and what it does not

Scope of the boil-out solver

Modeled: Lumped-capacitance bath energy balance (forward-Euler, 5-second steps); coil-side Dittus-Boelter + Schmidt helical correction; pool-boiling shell side at instantaneous bath T (linear R-717 correlation calibrated −25 to +30 °F, Mostinski-scaled for other refrigerants); heat-loss to ambient through insulated vessel shell; asymptote detection; bath cp updated at current T each step.

Not modeled: Two-phase coil-side flow (condensing in hot-gas mode); bath vaporization mass loss during cycle (constant bath mass assumed); transient pressure on the bath side; non-condensables; oil-layer effect on shell-side ho; coil flooding/draining transient at cycle start/end; valve-actuation delay; insulation degradation over time.

Practical interpretation: HELIX gives a useful upper-bound cycle time. The real installation will reach target as fast or faster, with margin from condensation enhancement in hot-gas mode. Validate the first installation by measuring actual cycle time and feeding the data back.

v.  Field Notes for Heat Recovery Service

Reference · 05

Service-specific guidance for heat-recovery coils. What HELIX models, what it does not, and what the field technician must specify before fabrication and commissioning.

Service overview

What heat recovery means here

A secondary fluid (water or glycol mixture) circulates through the coil tubes and picks up heat from the boiling refrigerant pool. Reject refrigeration enthalpy becomes useful makeup heat for plant wash-down, defrost, snow melt, or process pre-heat. Heat flows IN THE OPPOSITE DIRECTION from a subcooler — the bath is the WARM side here.

Direction guard

The bath must be warmer than the tube inlet temperature. If bath ≤ inlet, heat flows out of the tube (subcooling), not in — that is the Subcooler tab's domain. HELIX refuses to size a heat-recovery coil in the wrong direction.
Tube-side fluid selection

Water (closed loop)

Highest cp (1.0 Btu/lb·°F) and k → highest duty per pound. Use only on closed loops kept above freezing at all times. Inhibited corrosion package required (nitrite or molybdate). Typical fouling factor: 0.0005 hr·ft²·°F/Btu for clean closed loops with monitored chemistry.

30% / 50% Ethylene Glycol

Freeze-protected loops. 30% glycol is good to ~ +5 °F freeze point; 50% to ~ −34 °F. Both reduce cp and k vs. water, and dramatically increase viscosity — expect higher ΔP and lower hi. Required if any loop branch can be exposed to subfreezing ambient. Use a slightly higher fouling allowance (0.0007 hr·ft²·°F/Btu) due to glycol degradation over time.

Velocity guidelines

3 to 7 ft/s per IIAR-style liquid range. Below 3 ft/s, fouling accelerates and hi drops; above 7 ft/s, erosion-corrosion risk on long-duration service rises. Heat recovery coils often run continuously, so erosion margin matters more than for periodic-service coils.
Shell-side physics

Free convection — not pool boiling

The shell-side mechanism here is natural-convection cooling of the warm refrigerant pool against the colder tube wall (Churchill-Chu 1975 horizontal-cylinder correlation), NOT pool boiling. The bath does not boil at the tube wall in heat-recovery service because the tube wall is colder than the bath; the pool boils at the suction-pressure free surface. HELIX iteratively converges the outer-wall temperature so the natural-convection ΔT is consistent with the duty.

This is the most important physics correction in HELIX. Earlier versions of this calculation used the pool-boiling correlation here and overstated recovered duty by ~2× for typical scenarios. Confidence: high. Validity: RaD ∈ [10⁻⁵, 10¹²], which covers all practical coil geometries.

Partial submergence

If the coil top pokes above the normal liquid level (net submergence < 0 after the 6″ swell allowance), exposed turns see refrigerant vapor instead of liquid. Vapor-side ho at typical R-717 bath pressures is 2–5 Btu/hr·ft²·°F — essentially insulation (denser halocarbon/CO₂ vapor runs somewhat higher, still far below the liquid side). HELIX area-weights the effective ho when this happens and shows the submerged fraction prominently in the readout. Avoid this by sizing the vessel S/S length or coil pitch so the coil stays submerged at all operating levels.
System integration

Downstream load

Recovered duty is energy taken FROM the bath — meaning the refrigeration system sees a smaller net suction load by exactly that amount. If recovered duty is significant (≥ 5 TR), the system suction superheat may drop noticeably and compressor capacity headroom should be re-checked. Sizing the recovery loop too aggressively can starve the suction return.

Bypass and freeze protection

Coil tubes sit in a refrigerant bath ~−25 to +30 °F most of the time. The secondary fluid must not freeze in the tubes during low-flow conditions. Required:
  • Bypass loop with proportional control to maintain minimum tube velocity ≥ 3 ft/s at all times
  • Low-flow alarm and shut-off interlock
  • If water (not glycol), the closed-loop pump cannot stop while bath is cold — secondary heat source needed for outages
Material & code

Pipe material

For bath T down to −20 °F: ASTM A106 Gr. B carbon steel is acceptable. Below −20 °F: switch to A333 Gr. 6 with Charpy impact testing. All welds NDT to 100% per IIAR 2.

Verify pipe MAWP separately per ASME B31.5 and AHJ. Tube-side runs at glycol/water-loop pressure (typically 30–100 psig), usually well below refrigerant-side pressures (dramatically so for R-744), but the wall sees the differential. HELIX does not compute or certify pipe pressure rating.

What HELIX models — and what it does not

Scope of the heat-recovery solver

Modeled: Steady-state NTU-effectiveness with the boiling refrigerant pool at constant saturation T (no shell-side T glide — note zeotropic blends like R-407C really do glide); Dittus-Boelter tube side (n = 0.4, heating) + Schmidt helical correction; Churchill-Chu natural convection on the shell side with iteratively-converged outer-wall T; partial-submergence area-weighting; Ito pressure drop; water / 30% glycol / 50% glycol property tables fit to ASHRAE.

Not modeled: Boiling-induced agitation enhancement of the shell-side ho beyond Churchill-Chu (real coils may run slightly better than the prediction when pool boiling at the free surface is vigorous); transient start-up; freezing of tube-side fluid; secondary loop hydraulics outside the coil; bypass-loop control behavior; long-term glycol degradation effects on hi.

Practical interpretation: HELIX gives a conservative estimate of recovered duty in heat-recovery service. Real installations should match or slightly exceed predictions. If actual measured duty is significantly higher than predicted, the bath is likely more agitated than the natural-convection model captures — useful information but does not invalidate the design margin.
